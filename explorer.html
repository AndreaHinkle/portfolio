The Explorer

Project: The Explorer - Email newsletter redesign

Click to enlarge

Team members:

  • Creative director, Writer, Designer (myself), and Marketing Director
  • My role: Design and technical. I answered the questions “What are we capable of doing?” and “How can we do it?”. I also drafted the new look of the newsletter - with the exception of the logo, which was done by our creative director.
Situation:
  • We sent out an issue of the Explorer for each of our communities, with each community having a separate account with our email service provider. This was setup to save us money, as each of the accounts had a small enough user base to be available at the free pricing tier. Unfortunately it caused a lot of effort duplication on my part, in addition to a lot of unnecessary logging in and out.
  • We needed a way to nurture our marketing prospects, however the act of sending mailings to them, with all of their different accounts, was tedious. A paid account would open up automation options in our email service provider. I needed to revamp the process and decided to give the newsletter a facelift in the process.
Action:
  • I convinced marketing to pay for a master account, and brought the mailing lists into it, (on a trial basis).
  • I also applied a cleaner, more modern design to eliminate the sidebar for mobile readers, and streamlined the amount of content available in the newsletter to focus on our message. The idea being that having one full, short article to read before we asked them to take an action would make users more likely to read our community article.
  • People who opened our original email were sent a followup article a week later, to gage interest.
Results:
  • On average, 66% of people open the followup email. These names are then sent back to sales.
  • Hard bounces were sent back to sales as a way to reach out and update their records.
  • In the previous designs, most clicks were on the header graphic. This sent them to the page for the community sending the email, due to misleading graphics that looked like article headers. After the redesign, the majority of clicks are on the link to read the article.

Thanks for reading! - Andrea
